### 1.2 Fractal Mathematics Foundations

**Definition:**
A fractal is a geometric shape with self-similarity at different scales and typically has a non-integer (fractal) dimension.

**Key Properties:**
1. **Self-similarity:** Pattern repeats at different scales
2. **Scale invariance:** Properties unchanged under magnification
3. **Fractal dimension:** D_f ≠ integer (e.g., Sierpinski triangle: D = log(3)/log(2) ≈ 1.585)
4. **Infinite detail:** Complexity at all scales

**Bibliographic References:**

- Barnsley, M. F. (2014). *Fractals Everywhere* (3rd ed.). Dover Publications.

## 2. Famous Fractals

### 2.1 Mathematical Fractals

**Mandelbrot Set:**
- z_{n+1} = z_n² + c
- Infinitely complex boundary
- Self-similar at all scales

**Julia Sets:**
- Family of fractals related to Mandelbrot
- Different parameter c produces different Julia set

**Sierpinski Triangle:**
- Remove center triangle recursively
- Dimension: log(3)/log(2) ≈ 1.585

**Koch Snowflake:**
- Infinite perimeter, finite area
- Dimension: log(4)/log(3) ≈ 1.262

**Bibliographic References:**
- Mandelbrot, B. B. (1980). "Fractal aspects of the iteration of z → λz(1-z)". *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 357(1), 249-259.
- Peitgen, H. O., et al. (2004). *Chaos and Fractals: New Frontiers of Science* (2nd ed.). Springer.

## 4. Fractal Dimension

### 4.1 Definition

**Box-Counting Dimension:**
D = lim(ε→0) [log(N(ε)) / log(1/ε)]

Where N(ε) is number of boxes of size ε needed to cover the fractal.

**Examples:**
- Line: D = 1
- Square: D = 2
- Sierpinski triangle: D ≈ 1.585
- Coastline of Britain: D ≈ 1.25

**Bibliographic References:**
- Hausdorff, F. (1918). "Dimension und äußeres Maß". *Mathematische Annalen*, 79(1-2), 157-179.
- Mandelbrot, B. B. (1967). "How long is the coast of Britain?". *Science*, 156(3775), 636-638.

## 6. Fractals in Data Compression

### 6.1 Fractal Compression

**Concept:**
- Exploit self-similarity for compression
- Partition image, find similar sub-regions
- Store transformations instead of data

**Advantages:**
- High compression ratios possible
- Resolution-independent

**Disadvantages:**
- Slow encoding
- Loss of detail
- Patent restrictions (historically)

**Bibliographic References:**
